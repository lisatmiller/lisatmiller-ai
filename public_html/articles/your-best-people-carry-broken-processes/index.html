Your Best People Carry Your Most Broken Processes

They absorb the rework, the workarounds, and the friction. The cost is invisible. Here is why it is larger than you think and what AI changes.

Your best people carry your most broken processes because they are the ones who can figure out how to make a broken workflow produce results. They create workarounds, they fill the gaps, and they absorb the friction. The cost is invisible because it is built into their day, not billed as a line item. The better your people are, the longer broken processes survive, because your people make them work despite the breakage.

Why do your best people carry your most broken processes?

Every health system has processes that do not work the way they were designed. The gaps get filled by people. Not just any people. Your best people. The ones who are sharp enough to see where the process breaks, resourceful enough to build a workaround, and committed enough to keep the workflow producing results despite the breakage.

This is the hidden tax of operational excellence. Your best people make broken processes work, which means the broken processes never become visible enough to fix. The process produces results because someone is carrying it. Leadership sees the results and assumes the process is fine. The person carrying it burns out, and the cycle continues.

I saw this for 30 years inside hospital operations. I watched teams carry processes that should have been redesigned years earlier. I watched what happened when those processes were finally fixed. The capacity returned was not a theoretical number. It was hours that went back to the work people actually went into healthcare to do.

How much does it cost when good people absorb broken workflows?

Approximately 25 percent of hospital spending is administration, not care. That number does not appear on a single budget line. It is distributed across every department, every workflow, every person who is doing work that exists only because a process breaks somewhere upstream.

~25%
Of hospital spending is administration, not care
Himmelstein et al., Health Affairs, 2014

The cost shows up in places you do not think to look. The revenue cycle specialist who spends three hours a day reworking claims that should have been clean on submission. The nurse who retypes patient demographics between three systems that were never connected. The supply chain manager who manually reconciles invoices against contracts because no one built the system to do it automatically.

None of that work was in the job description. None of it was budgeted. None of it was approved. It accumulated because systems were added without being connected and processes were patched without being redesigned. Your best people absorbed it because someone had to.

What happens when AI absorbs the work your best people are carrying?

When AI absorbs the rework and workarounds, your best people get their capacity back. That is the return most CFOs underestimate because it does not show up as a line-item savings. It shows up as time. Time redirected from manual work to patient care. Time redirected from rework to prevention. Time redirected from data entry to analysis and decision-making.

The goal is capacity returned, not headcount removed. AI absorbs the work nobody went into healthcare to do, and your people are trained to run and supervise what gets built. Adoption and change management are part of every deployment, because staff resistance kills more AI projects than technology does.

When your best people see the tool absorbing the work they never wanted to do in the first place, they become its strongest advocates. When they feel threatened rather than supported, they will quietly ensure the tool does not work. The difference is in how the deployment is framed and who owns it inside your organization.

How do you identify which processes your best people are carrying?

You ask them. Not in a survey. In a conversation. The question is simple: what work are you doing that should not exist? Your best people know exactly where the breakage is because they are the ones patching it. They will tell you, if you ask, and if the answer does not threaten their job.

The assessment process maps where manual effort concentrates in your organization. It prices the work in dollars and hours. It matches proven AI capability to each workflow. And it sequences the opportunities by expected return, so the first deployment proves value inside 90 days and builds momentum for the next one.

The bottom line

Your best people are carrying your most broken processes, and the cost is invisible because it is absorbed by their time. AI absorbs that work, and your people get the capacity back. The technology exists. The proof exists. The question is whether your organization maps the work, installs the tools, and measures the results, or continues letting your best people subsidize your broken processes with their own time.
